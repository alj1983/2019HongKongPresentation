Pretentious flowers, colorful plumage, and attractive ornaments—all are created with the ultimate goal to attract the opposite sex and contribute half of one’s own genes to the next generation. Recombination of genetic material during sexual reproduction allows the next generation to cope with changing selection pressures. Thus, genetic variation is considered the key to survival of populations in the long term3, and is recognized as a main target for the conservation of biological diversity. In contrast, lack of genetic variation is regarded as an evolutionary dead-end . For example, conservation management follows the rough guideline that more than 1 000 genetically different individuals in a population should mate randomly to avoid inbreeding depression, and retain evolutionary potential.

Asexual reproduction in flowering plants

coursehero.com

Yet, many species are sexually abstinent and produce clonal offspring that are genetically identical. While more rare in animals, roughly 40% of all plants can reproduce asexually, mostly through clonal growth where parental genotypes (genets) grow vegetative modules (ramets) that often remain connected via underground stolons or rhizomes.

Asexual reproduction in seagrass

Fonseca & Uhrin (2009), Marine Fisheries Review

While clones benefit from resource sharing (clonal integration) 14 , niche specialization, and rapid vegetative growth 14 , they are predicted to survive only for short periods and in stable environments 6 due to the accumulation of deleterious mutations, slow rates of genetic evolution, and the lack of DNA repair mechanisms afforded by meiosis (Muller’s ratchet, mutational meltdown) 7–9 .
However, seagrasses form large clonal meadows that can persist for millenia. For example clonal meadows of the mediterranean seagrass Posidonia oceanica was estimated more than 10,000 years old.
Here we see a clonal meadow of the seagqrass Zostera marina in the Baltic sea. the evolutionary success of massive-ancient seagrass clones challenges the direct relationship between genetic diversity, adaptation potential, and persistence.
