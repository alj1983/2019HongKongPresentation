The resulting genetic variation is considered the key to survival of populations in the long term, and is recognized as a main target for the conservation of biological diversity. In contrast, lack of genetic variation is regarded as an evolutionary dead-end . For example, conservation management follows the rough guideline that more than 1 000 genetically different individuals in a population should mate randomly to avoid inbreeding depression, and retain evolutionary potential. Asexual reproduction in flowering plants

40% of plants reproduce clonally

coursehero.com

While more rare in animals, roughly 40% of all plants can reproduce asexually, mostly through clonal growth where parental genotypes (genets) grow vegetative modules (ramets) that often remain connected via underground stolons or rhizomes. While clones benefit from resource sharing and rapid rapid vegetative growth 14 , they are predicted to survive only for short periods and in stable environments 6 due to the accumulation of deleterious mutations and slow rates of genetic evolution. However, individual clonal plants feature some of our most important crops 13 (e.g. garlic, hops, potatoes, and turmeric) and many of the earth’s most invasive 10–12 , and oldest plants. For example

>200,000 years old seagrass meadow

a clonal meadow of the seagrass Posidonia oceanica in Australia has been estimated to be more than 200,000 years old. Such large, old clones challenge the direct relationship between genetic diversity and long-term survival, and suggest that genes may not be the single most important components of diversity and persistence.

Compensation for absence of genetic variation

Methylation variation increased productivity and resistance

Arabidopsis thaliana

Latzel et al. (2013), Nature Communications

In Arabidopsis it was shown that methylation variation, a level of epigenetic variation, can compensate for the lack of genetic variation. Genetically identical plants were grown together in a pot. They differed in their epigenotype. Sixteen different epigenotypes showed increased productivity and stability as compared to the case when all plants had the same epigenotype. Especially when pathogens and competitors were present.

Epigenetic variation

Allis et al. (2015), Epigenetics

The insight that epigenetic variation can provide a non-genetic component of heritable adaptive variation is a fairly new concept in the fields of ecology and evolution 18–21. Epigenetic modifications, including histone modifications, non-coding RNA and DNA methylation do not alter the DNA sequence but do affect gene expression and, in that way, can organize one and the same genome in different ways. For example, all cells of our body have the same genotype but are phenotypically quite different, because they differ epigenetically.

What is the methylome?

Zakhari (2013), Alcohol research: current reviews

The methylome is the set of DNA methylation modifications in an organism's genome. The genome consists of four nucleotides AGCT, and C, the cytosine can be mehtylated, when a methyl-group is attached to its pyrimidine ring by DNA methyltransferase. This occurs in sequence contexts, such as when a c is followed by a g. Although it does not alter the DNA or protein sequence, it alters the expression of genes

DNA methylation often represses gene expression

source: http://cnx.org/contents/QcTHfqRM@1/Basic-Principles-of-Genetics

DNA methylation often represses transcription when it occurs in promotor regions of genes. It also represses transcription in TEs, also called jumping genes. When DNA is methylated, a chain of reaction takes place by which DNA is more compactly organized around the histones, and less accessible to transcrition enzymes.

DNA methylation heritability

source: http://www.atdbio.com/content/56/Epigenetics

From an evolutionary perspective, DNA methylation is the most relevant epigenetic mechanism as it can be transgenerationally stable when maintained during mitosis and meiosis and mitosis by methyltransferase enzymnes. Methylation patterns in the genome are generally reset during meiosis and embryogenesis. After a certain stage of embryogenesis, when an embryo grows and cells are dividing, mitosis takes place. Newly synthesized DNA strands are then methylated at the same locations as the parental DNA strands. So the methylation status is kept constant over mitotic cell divisions. But the methylation state can change spontaneously - at a 64,000 times faster spontaneous epimutation rate than mutation rate. Moreover, methylation states change in response to environmental changes/stresses.
  • Epimutation rate ca. 64,000 x genetic mutation rate
  • Response to environment

Resetting versus memory in DNA methylation

Crisp et al. (2016), Science Advances

Now when we expose a plant to dehydration and light stress, part of the methylation changes are reset when the stress is removed, but part of the methylation response also remains and imprints this plant and further generations to dry and bright conditions. In this way it can rapidly adapt epigenetically to environmental change.

Asexual reproduction in seagrass

Fonseca & Uhrin (2009), Marine Fisheries Review

Clonal propagation is especially well-developed in aquatic plants. Seagrasses, the only marine plants can reproduce sexually and vegetatively so that each shoot shares the same genotype. In seagrass meadows, the functional role of epigenetic variation is not only of fundamental interest but also of applied interest for sustainable management and restoration of these important ecosystem engineers.
Seagrasses form the foundational basis of some of the most productive and highly diverse coastal marine ecosystems on the planet and provide ecosystem services such as carbon sequestration,

Distribution of Zostera marina

From IUCN Red List of Threatened Species. Version 2010.4

In this study, we aimed to characterize the functional relevance of epigenetic variation for stress resilience in the seagrass Z. marina. Z. marina is the most widely distributed seagrass in the northern hemisphere, and grows in highly contrasting habitats from Arctic to sub-tropical waters. Methylome characterization

Wang et al. (2015), Open Biology

The methylome was characterized by MethylRAD sequencing. MethylRAD is a genome-reduction method based on a methylation-dependent restriction enzyme that targets CG and CHG motifs. The enzyme cuts out 32bp fragments surrounding methylated sites. These digested fragments were sequenced on an Illumina NextSeq (1x75bp) and mapped to the in-silico digested Z. marina genome.

Basic results

Pairwise methylation differences

Pairwise methylation differences (methylated/not methylated) between clonal shoots did not differ from pairwise methylation differences between the 39 clonal shoots and the 1 shoot that had a different genotype. On average 23% of MethylRAD tags were differently methylated between the samples. Differences were generally lower in CHG as compared with CG sequence contexts.

Conclusions

Methylation variation creates phenotypic diversity
and may explain long-term survival of clonal seagrass meadows lacking genetic variation

By creating functional phenotypic diversity Thus, the current reliance on allelic and genotypic variation alone for management and conservation may be too narrow to caputre stress reslience, stability and adaptive potential of clonal plants. Given that 40% of all plants reproduce clonally, our findings are important to other fields, such as invasion biology and crop breeding strategies. This is directly relevant for management programs of clonal organisms designed to assess evolutionary potential and population stability, and to minimize the loss of biodiversity.

Net loss of seagrass area

Waycott et al. (2009); PNAS

Nearly one-third of global seagrass area has disappeared over the last 100 years- this is equivalent to losing a seagrass meadow of a football pitch size every half an hour. The rate of loss accelerated from about 1 percent per year (1 football field every half an hour) before 1940 to 7 percent per year since 1990 (1 football field every 4 minutes). Reasons besides coastal development and pollution are rising temperatures, hitting the meadows in form of heatwaves.

Carbon sequestration (48-160 Tg C yr-1)

Photo credit: Oscar Serrano, https://www.eurekalert.org/multimedia/pub/165345.php

Carbon-sequestration: Long term storage of carbon to mitigate global warming. Organic carbon stores beneath seagrass meadows in Shark Bay. Organic material derived from seagrass roots, rhizomes and leaf sheaths is embedded in sandy sediments, forming organic deposits several meters in thickness. Loss of seagrass meadows means that they actually become carbon sources via the remobilization of carbon stocks accumulated over thousands of years. 9000 Tg C were emitted in year 2010.
